Radioactivity (or radioactive decay) is a spontaneous process in which an unstable parent nucleus transforms into a more stable configuration through the emission of particles or electromagnetic radiation. The resulting daughter nucleus may itself be unstable and continue to decay until stability is achieved.

A radioactive substance containing atoms with the same nuclear structure is referred to as a radioactive nuclide or radionuclide. Radioactive nuclides may occur naturally or be artificially produced through nuclear reactions.

Several types of radioactive decay exist, depending on the characteristics of the nuclide. The decay modes of greatest relevance to medical physics are alpha decay, beta decay, and gamma emission (Podgoršak, 2016).

## Alpha Decay

In alpha (α) decay, an unstable parent nucleus transforms into a more stable daughter nucleus by emitting an energetic alpha particle. The alpha particle is a helium nucleus consisting of two protons and two neutrons. As a result of this emission, the atomic number (Z) of the parent nucleus decreases by two, and the mass number (A) decreases by four.

As the α particle travels through matter, it interacts strongly with surrounding atoms, losing energy rapidly through ionization and excitation processes. After losing its kinetic energy, it captures two electrons from the surrounding medium and becomes a neutral helium atom. The daughter atom, initially left with an excess of electrons relative to its nuclear charge, subsequently releases these electrons and returns to a neutral state (Podgoršak, 2016).

## Beta Decay

Three processes fall into the category of beta (β) decay: beta minus (β−) decay, beta plus (β+) decay, and electron capture.

### Beta Minus Decay

In beta minus decay, a neutron-rich parent nucleus converts one of its neutrons into a proton and emits an electron (beta particle) along with an electron antineutrino. As a result, the atomic number of the daughter nucleus increases by one, while the mass number remains unchanged. This process occurs because the nucleus seeks a more stable neutron-to-proton ratio.

Antineutrinos (and neutrinos) are electrically neutral particles with extremely small mass and interact very weakly with matter (Podgoršak et al., 2014).

### Beta Plus Decay

In beta plus decay, a proton-rich parent nucleus converts one of its protons into a neutron and emits a positron (a positively charged electron) along with an electron neutrino. As a result, the atomic number decreases by one, while the mass number remains unchanged.

<img alt='Figure 8: Beta plus decay' src='/figures/8.png' />
*Figure 8: Beta plus decay.*

The emitted positron gradually loses its kinetic energy and eventually annihilates with an electron, producing two gamma photons of 511 keV energy, which are emitted in nearly opposite directions. This process forms the basis of positron emission tomography (PET) (Podgoršak et al., 2014).

### Electron Capture

Electron capture occurs when an inner-shell electron (usually from the K or L shell) is absorbed by the nucleus. The electron combines with a proton to form a neutron, and a neutrino is emitted. As a result, the atomic number decreases by one, while the mass number remains unchanged. The created vacancy is filled by an electron from a higher energy level, resulting in the emission of characteristic X-rays and/or Auger electrons (Cherry et al., 2012).

## Gamma Decay and Internal Conversion

Alpha and beta decay often leave the daughter nucleus in an excited state. The nucleus returns to its ground state through one of two processes.

The first process is gamma (γ) decay, in which the excess nuclear energy is emitted in the form of one or more photons. These photons are called gamma rays (γ-rays) to indicate their nuclear origin. The energy of the emitted photon corresponds to the difference between two nuclear energy states (Podgoršak, 2016).

<img alt='Figure 9: Gamma decay' src='/figures/9.png' />
*Figure 9: Gamma decay.*

The second process is internal conversion, in which the excited nucleus transfers its excess energy directly to an electron (typically from the K shell). This electron is then ejected from the atom. The resulting vacancy is filled by an electron from a higher energy level, producing characteristic X-rays and/or Auger electrons (Podgoršak et al., 2014).

In most alpha and beta decay events, gamma emission or internal conversion occurs almost instantaneously. However, in some cases, the nucleus remains in a metastable state and de-excites after a measurable delay (Podgoršak, 2016).

## Activity

Activity (A) is a quantity used to describe the intensity of a radioactive source. It is defined as the rate at which radioactive nuclei decay in a given sample.

The unit of activity is the becquerel (Bq), where:

**1 Bq = 1 decay per second**

In medical applications, activity levels are typically much higher and are commonly expressed in megabecquerels (MBq) or gigabecquerels (GBq), corresponding to millions or billions of decays per second.

Activity is directly related to the number of radioactive atoms present in a sample: the greater the number of unstable nuclei, the higher the activity (Cherry et al., 2012; Podgoršak, 2016).

### Exponential Law of Radioactive Decay

Radioactive decay is a random process at the level of individual atoms but follows a predictable pattern when large numbers of atoms are considered.

The exponential law of radioactive decay describes how the number of radioactive nuclei decreases over time:

**N(t) = N₀ e⁻λt**

According to this relation, the number of nuclei N(t) at time t is equal to the initial number of nuclei, N₀, multiplied by the exponential factor e⁻λt, which describes the characteristic exponential decrease of radioactive decay. The decay constant (λ) represents the probability per unit time that a nucleus will decay and is characteristic of each radionuclide (Cherry et al., 2012).

The half-life (t₁/₂) is the time required for half of the radioactive nuclei in a sample to decay.

For example, technetium-99m, which is widely used in nuclear medicine, has a half-life of approximately 6 hours. This means that if initially the number of radioactive nuclei is N₀, then:

- after 6 hours → 50% of N₀ remain
- after 12 hours → 25% of N₀ remain

Half-life is an important concept in radiation protection because it determines how long a radioactive substance remains hazardous.

Half-lives are characteristic properties of radioactive nuclides and vary widely, from fractions of a second to millions of years.

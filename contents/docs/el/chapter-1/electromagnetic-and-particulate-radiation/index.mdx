---
title: "Electromagnetic and Particulate Radiation"
description: "Definition and classification of radiation as electromagnetic or particulate, and properties of the electromagnetic spectrum."
---

Radiation may be defined as the emission and propagation of energy through space or a medium, either in the form of electromagnetic waves or particles. Depending on the mode of energy transfer, radiation is classified as either particulate or electromagnetic.

Particulate radiation consists of ions and subatomic particles, such as electrons, positrons, protons, and neutrons.

Electromagnetic radiation consists of oscillating electric and magnetic fields that are perpendicular to each other and to the direction of propagation. These oscillations occur in phase. Electromagnetic waves are characterized by their wavelength (λ) and frequency (ν). The wavelength is the distance between identical points on successive cycles, while the frequency represents the number of oscillations per unit time.

The speed of electromagnetic radiation is equal to the product of its frequency and wavelength and depends on the properties of the medium through which it propagates. In a vacuum, all electromagnetic waves travel at the same constant speed (the speed of light), independent of frequency.

When electromagnetic radiation interacts with matter, it behaves as discrete packets of energy known as photons. The energy (E) of a photon is proportional to its frequency and is given by:

**E = hν**

where h is Planck's constant.

The electromagnetic spectrum classifies radiation according to frequency (or wavelength) and energy. It spans a wide range, from low-energy radio waves to high-energy X-rays and gamma rays, and includes microwaves, infrared radiation, visible light, and ultraviolet radiation. X-rays and gamma rays are particularly important in radiation protection because their energies are sufficient to ionize matter, break molecular bonds, and damage genetic material (Hosny et al., 2017; Ng & Dance, 2014).

---
title: Dose Limits
description: Regulatory dose limits for occupational and public exposure, and comparison with common medical procedure doses.
---

Dose limits are established to protect individuals from excessive radiation exposure and to reduce the risk of harmful health effects. They are based on recommendations from the ICRP and have been incorporated into European legislation through Council Directive 2013/59/Euratom.

Dose limits apply to occupational and public exposure in planned exposure situations. They do not apply to medical exposure of patients, since these exposures are justified by clinical benefit. Dose limits are not thresholds for harm, but regulatory tools designed to control avoidable exposure and ensure that radiation risks remain low.

The dose limits for occupational and public exposure are summarized below.

| Type of limit | Occupational | Public |
|---|---|---|
| Effective Dose | 20 mSv/year (averaged over 5 years, max 50 mSv in a single year) | 1 mSv/year |
| Lens of the eye | 20 mSv/year (averaged over 5 years, max 50 mSv in a single year) | 15 mSv/year |
| Skin | 500 mSv/year (averaged over 1 cm²) | 50 mSv/year (averaged over 1 cm²) |
| Extremities (hands/feet) | 500 mSv/year | Not applicable |

Pregnant workers should be employed under conditions in which the equivalent dose to the unborn child is kept as low as reasonably achievable and, in any case, does not exceed 1 mSv for the remainder of the pregnancy. Breastfeeding workers should not be employed in tasks involving a significant risk of radionuclide intake or bodily contamination (Council of the European Union, 2013).

For comparison with dose limits, indicative average values of effective dose from common medical procedures in Europe are presented below.

- Chest X-ray: ~0.1 mSv
- Mammography: ~0.3 mSv
- CT scan (chest): ~6.6 mSv
- CT scan (head): ~1.9 mSv
- CT scan (abdomen): ~11.3 mSv
- Interventional procedures: may range from a few mSv to several tens of mSv (European Commission, 2014)

Natural background radiation: ~2-3 mSv per year (UNSCEAR, 2008).

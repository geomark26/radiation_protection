---
title: Fundamental Principles of Radiation Protection
description: "The three ICRP principles: justification, optimization (ALARA), and dose limitation."
---

The system of radiation protection is based on three fundamental principles established by the ICRP: justification, optimization, and dose limitation. These principles provide a structured approach to ensuring that ionizing radiation is used safely and responsibly.

Justification states that any decision involving exposure to ionizing radiation should result in more benefit than harm. In practice, this means that a procedure or activity involving radiation should only be performed if it provides a clear advantage, such as improving diagnosis or treatment. This principle applies to all exposure categories, including medical, occupational, and public exposure.

Optimization of protection, commonly known as the ALARA principle (As Low As Reasonably Achievable), requires that radiation exposures be kept as low as reasonably achievable while still achieving the intended objective. This involves minimizing the radiation dose, the number of individuals exposed, and the duration and likelihood of exposure, while taking into account practical factors such as available technology, economic considerations, and clinical needs. This principle is particularly important in everyday clinical practice.

Dose limitation sets upper limits on radiation exposure for individuals in order to prevent unacceptable risks. Dose limits apply to occupational and public exposure, but not to patients undergoing medical procedures, since their exposure is justified by direct health benefits. These limits ensure that no individual is exposed to unnecessarily high levels of radiation under normal conditions (Council of the European Union, 2013; ICRP, 2007).

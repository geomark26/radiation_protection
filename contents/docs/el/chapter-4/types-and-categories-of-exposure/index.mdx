---
title: Types and Categories of Exposure
description: Classification of radiation exposure by situation type and by who is exposed.
---

According to ICRP Publication 103 (2007), exposure to ionizing radiation is classified in two complementary ways: into exposure situations and into exposure categories. This classification helps determine how radiation protection measures are applied in different contexts.

## Types of Exposure Situations

Exposure situations describe how and when exposure occurs.

Planned exposure situations are those that are deliberately introduced and managed in advance. These include both anticipated exposures (normal exposures) and unanticipated exposures (potential exposures). Normal exposures include routine uses of ionizing radiation, such as diagnostic radiology, interventional procedures, radiotherapy, nuclear medicine, and industrial applications. Potential exposures may involve unintended overexposures resulting from equipment malfunction, software or hardware failure, or irradiation of the wrong patient.

Emergency exposure situations arise from unexpected events that require urgent action to reduce exposure. Examples include nuclear accidents, loss of radioactive sources, or malicious use of radioactive material.

Existing exposure situations refer to exposures that are already present when a decision is made to control them. Typical examples include natural background radiation (such as radon) and residual contamination from past nuclear events (ICRP, 2007).

## Categories of Exposure

Exposure categories describe who is exposed.

Occupational exposure refers to radiation exposure of workers incurred as a result of their work and from situations that can reasonably be regarded as their responsibility. Healthcare workers involved in procedures using ionizing radiation are considered occupationally exposed and have specific rights and responsibilities in relation to radiological protection.

Medical exposure refers to exposure of patients undergoing diagnostic or therapeutic procedures involving ionizing radiation. In these cases, exposure is intentionally delivered for the direct health benefit of the patient. Medical exposure also includes exposure of carers and comforters, as well as participants in biomedical research.

Public exposure refers to exposure of individuals from all other sources, excluding occupational and medical exposure. This includes exposure from environmental sources or incidental presence near radiation practices.

For the purposes of radiation protection, non-radiation healthcare workers are generally considered members of the public, as their exposure is incidental and not part of their professional duties (ICRP, 2007).

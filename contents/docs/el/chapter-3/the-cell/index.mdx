The cell is the fundamental structural and functional unit of living organisms. Eukaryotic cells are composed of three main components: the cell membrane, the cytoplasm, and the nucleus.

The cytoplasm is a gel-like substance composed primarily of water, proteins, sugars, and salts. It contains organelles such as ribosomes, which are responsible for protein synthesis, and mitochondria, which play a key role in energy production. It also includes the cytoskeleton, a network of protein fibers that maintains cell shape and facilitates intracellular transport. The cytoplasm is the site of numerous metabolic processes essential for cellular function.

The cell membrane separates the cell from its external environment and regulates the exchange of substances and signals.

The nucleus is a membrane-bound organelle that contains the genetic material in the form of deoxyribonucleic acid (DNA), which carries the information required for most cellular processes (Alberts et al., 2013).

## DNA Structure

DNA is a large molecule composed of two antiparallel strands forming a double helix. Each strand consists of repeating units called nucleotides, which include a sugar, a phosphate group, and a nitrogenous base. The nucleotides are linked by phosphodiester bonds, forming the backbone of the DNA molecule.

The four bases, adenine, thymine, cytosine, and guanine, pair in a complementary manner, with adenine pairing with thymine and cytosine pairing with guanine. The two strands are held together by hydrogen bonds between these complementary bases.

In the nucleus, DNA is organized as chromatin, a complex of DNA and proteins (histones). During cell division, chromatin condenses to form chromosomes (Reindl et al., 2023; Alberts et al., 2013).

## Types of Cells

Human cells are classified as either somatic cells or germ cells. Somatic cells divide by mitosis, producing two genetically identical daughter cells, whereas germ cells divide by meiosis, producing reproductive cells with half the genetic material.

Somatic cells may also be described according to their level of differentiation. Stem cells are undifferentiated and capable of self-renewal and differentiation. Transit (amplifying) cells are partially differentiated and divide a limited number of times, while mature cells are fully differentiated and perform specific functions within tissues. Cells that divide rapidly and are less differentiated tend to be more radiosensitive (Suntharalingam et al., 2005).

## Cell Cycle

The cell cycle consists of two main phases: the M phase and interphase. The M phase includes mitosis, during which the nucleus divides, followed by cytokinesis, resulting in the formation of two daughter cells. Interphase is the period between divisions, during which the cell grows, performs its functions, and prepares for division.

Interphase is subdivided into three stages: G1, S, and G2. During G1, the cell grows and synthesizes proteins; during the S phase, DNA replication occurs; and during G2, the cell continues to grow and prepares for mitosis.

Cells possess regulatory checkpoints that monitor internal conditions and DNA integrity before allowing progression to the next phase. If DNA damage occurs, for example due to ionizing radiation, the cell cycle may be temporarily halted to allow repair. If the damage is too extensive, the cell may undergo programmed cell death (Alberts et al., 2013).

---
title: "Factors Affecting Radiation Damage"
description: "Physical and biological factors that determine the extent of radiation-induced biological damage."
---

The extent of biological damage caused by ionizing radiation depends on a variety of physical and biological factors. These include the radiation dose and dose rate, the type of radiation, the characteristics of the exposed cells, as well as individual factors such as age and overall health status.

- Higher doses result in more extensive cellular damage and a greater probability of biological effects
- At low dose rates, cells have enough time to repair damage between successive energy deposition events. In contrast, at high dose rates, energy is delivered over a short period, allowing damage to accumulate more rapidly and increasing the likelihood of severe and complex lesions
- Low-LET radiation produces sparsely distributed ionizations and primarily simpler, more easily repairable DNA damage, whereas high-LET radiation produces dense ionization and complex, less easily repairable damage, with an increased likelihood of cell death or mutations
- Cells are more radiosensitive during the G2 and M phases of the cell cycle. In contrast, they exhibit greater resistance during the G1 phase and particularly during the late S phase, partly due to active DNA synthesis and more efficient DNA repair mechanisms
- Cells that divide rapidly and are less differentiated, such as stem cells, germ cells, and hematopoietic cells, are generally more radiosensitive. In contrast, highly differentiated cells with little or no capacity for division, such as neurons and muscle cells, are more radioresistant. Cells with intermediate characteristics, such as epithelial and endothelial cells, show moderate radiosensitivity
- Children are more radiosensitive than adults due to higher rates of cell division and a longer expected lifespan, which increases the likelihood that radiation-induced effects may develop over time (Baeyens et al., 2023; Cherry, 2012; Saha, 2019)

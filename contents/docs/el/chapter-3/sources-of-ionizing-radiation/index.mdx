Human beings are continuously exposed to low levels of ionizing radiation from both natural and artificial sources. Natural sources have always been present in the environment, whereas artificial sources have been introduced through human activities. With the increasing use of ionizing radiation in medicine, industry, and research, radiation protection has become essential (UNSCEAR, 2008).

## Natural Background Radiation

Natural background radiation arises from several sources and constitutes the largest contribution to the average annual radiation exposure of the population, accounting for approximately 80% of total exposure. These sources are commonly categorized as terrestrial radiation, inhalation, ingestion, and cosmic radiation (UNSCEAR, 2008).

Terrestrial radiation originates from naturally occurring radionuclides in the Earth's crust, primarily uranium-238, thorium-232, and potassium-40, along with their decay products. Their concentrations vary depending on local geology, resulting in geographical differences in exposure. These radionuclides may also be present in building materials, contributing to indoor exposure.

Inhalation exposure is mainly associated with radioactive gases such as radon-222 and thoron-220, produced from the decay of uranium and thorium in soil and rocks. Radon is the most significant contributor to natural radiation exposure. While it is diluted in outdoor air, it can accumulate indoors, especially in poorly ventilated spaces.

Ingestion of radionuclides occurs through food and water. Naturally occurring radionuclides are taken up by plants and enter the food chain. Radionuclides such as carbon-14 and potassium-40 are naturally present in the human body and contribute to internal exposure.

Cosmic radiation originates from outer space and consists of high-energy particles and electromagnetic radiation. When it interacts with the Earth's atmosphere, secondary radiation is produced. The atmosphere provides significant shielding; therefore, radiation levels increase with altitude and are higher at aircraft cruising altitudes (UNSCEAR, 2008; Baeyens et al., 2023).

## Artificial Radiation Sources

Artificial sources of ionizing radiation arise from human activities and contribute approximately 20% of the average annual radiation exposure. These include medical applications, nuclear power generation, industrial and research uses, and, to a lesser extent, nuclear accidents and past atmospheric nuclear testing (UNSCEAR, 2008).

Among these, medical exposure represents by far the largest contribution. Ionizing radiation is widely used in diagnostic imaging and therapy, with doses varying depending on the type and complexity of the procedure. Medical exposure is not evenly distributed across the population, as some individuals may undergo repeated or high-dose procedures. In developed healthcare systems, the average dose from medical exposure may approach or even equal that from natural background radiation (UNSCEAR, 2008; Baeyens et al., 2023).

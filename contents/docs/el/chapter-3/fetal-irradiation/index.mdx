---
title: "Fetal Irradiation"
description: "Radiation sensitivity of the developing embryo and fetus at different stages of pregnancy."
---

Radiation exposure during pregnancy requires special consideration because the developing embryo and fetus are particularly radiosensitive. This increased sensitivity is due to rapid cell division, a high proportion of undifferentiated cells, and critical periods of organ development. The effects of radiation depend mainly on the absorbed dose and the stage of development at the time of exposure.

- During the pre-implantation stage (approximately 1-10 days post-conception), radiation exposure typically results in an "all-or-nothing" effect. The embryo either dies or survives without detectable damage.
- During the organogenesis period (approximately 11 days to 8 weeks post-conception), the embryo is highly radiosensitive. Exposure during this stage may result in structural abnormalities, particularly affecting the central nervous system, skeleton, and developing organs. At sufficiently high doses, these effects may lead to embryonic or neonatal death.
- During the fetal period (after approximately 8 weeks post-conception), the risk of severe structural abnormalities decreases; however, other effects may occur, such as growth retardation and functional disorders, including intellectual disability (particularly with exposure between 8 and 15 weeks), as well as an increased risk of cancer later in life (Baeyens et al., 2023; Wondergem, 2014).

These effects form the basis for the implementation of strict radiation protection measures during pregnancy, with emphasis on avoiding unnecessary exposure and applying appropriate protective measures.

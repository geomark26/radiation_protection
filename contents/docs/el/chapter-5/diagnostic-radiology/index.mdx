Diagnostic radiology and image-guided interventional procedures use X-rays for imaging and, in some cases, treatment guidance. Since their discovery by Wilhelm Conrad Röntgen in 1895, X-rays have become essential in medical practice due to their ability to penetrate tissues and produce images of internal structures.

*Figure 25: Photograph of the first radiograph of the hand of Anna Bertha Röntgen, taken on December 22, 1895. From Wellcome Library, Wikimedia Commons. CC BY 4.0.*

Radiographic techniques provide static images of the body, while fluoroscopy allows real-time imaging, supporting both diagnosis and minimally invasive procedures. Common procedures of radiology include general radiography, computed tomography (CT), mammography, and interventional procedures such as angiography, stenting, and image-guided biopsy (IAEA, 2018).

## X-ray Production

Medical images are formed based on the different attenuation properties of tissues, which depend on their density and atomic composition.

X-rays are generated in an X-ray tube, where electrons emitted from a heated cathode are accelerated toward a metal anode. When these high-energy electrons interact with the target, X-rays are produced and exit through a window to form the primary beam.

As the X-ray beam passes through the patient, some photons are absorbed, some are scattered, and some are transmitted to form the image. Scattered radiation is emitted in different directions, making the patient the main source of secondary radiation in the room. Although scattered radiation is much less intense than the primary beam, it is the main source of occupational exposure, since healthcare workers are rarely exposed directly to the primary beam.

Within the X-ray tube, X-rays are emitted in all directions; therefore, the tube is externally shielded, except for the window, to prevent radiation from spreading in unwanted directions. Nevertheless, a small fraction of high-energy photons may penetrate the protective housing, resulting in leakage radiation (Nowotny, 2014; Poletti, 2014).

## Radiation Protection Measures in Radiography

Radiographic procedures are typically performed in dedicated X-ray rooms, where structural shielding provides adequate protection. Access to these rooms is restricted, and warning signs indicate the presence of ionizing radiation.

In certain situations, healthcare personnel may need to assist patients (e.g., elderly patients or children). In such cases, the following precautions should be observed:

- Wear appropriate protective equipment (e.g., a lead apron)
- Maintain the maximum possible distance from the X-ray beam
- Keep hands outside the primary beam
- Avoid repeated exposure of the same staff member
- Pregnancy or suspected pregnancy should be reported to the radiology department
- Follow the instructions of designated radiation protection personnel (IAEA, 2018; ICRP, 2013)

Mobile X-ray units are often used in areas such as intensive care units, emergency departments, or wards, where structural shielding may be limited. In these situations, the following precautions should be observed:

- Personnel not required near the patient should keep as far a distance as possible
- Those who must remain should wear appropriate protective equipment
- Use mobile shielding when available
- Follow the instructions of designated radiation protection personnel (IAEA, 2018)

## Radiation Protection Measures in Fluoroscopy

Fluoroscopy and image-guided interventional procedures are performed in controlled areas with restricted access, whereas mobile fluoroscopy systems (C-arms) are used in operating theatres and emergency settings.

<img alt='Figure 29: C-arm fluoroscopy system' src='/figures/29.png' />
*Figure 29: C-arm. From Mobile X-ray image intensifier, by Dennisthemonkeychild (2015), Wikimedia Commons. CC BY-SA 4.0.*

Since staff exposure is mainly due to scattered radiation from the patient, the following measures are essential:

- Remain in the room only when necessary
- Maximize distance from the patient during exposure
- Wear protective lead aprons
- Use additional protective equipment, such as thyroid shields, protective eyewear, and gloves, when appropriate
- Never place any part of the body in the primary beam
- Personnel who are or may be pregnant should inform the responsible supervisor
- Follow the instructions of designated radiation protection personnel (IAEA, 2018; ICRP, 2013)

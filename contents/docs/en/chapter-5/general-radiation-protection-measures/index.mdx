General radiation protection measures are based on simple and effective principles that can be applied in a wide range of situations encountered by healthcare personnel. Its application principles is essential for minimizing exposure and ensuring safe working conditions in environments where ionizing radiation is used.

## Time, Distance, and Shielding

Three fundamental strategies are used to reduce external radiation exposure: minimizing time, maximizing distance, and using appropriate shielding. The radiation dose received is directly proportional to the duration of exposure, assuming a constant dose rate; therefore, reducing the time spent near a radiation source decreases the accumulated dose.

Radiation intensity decreases with increasing distance from the source, following the inverse square law for point sources. Doubling the distance from the source reduces the exposure to approximately one quarter. Although this relationship is less exact for extended sources, increasing distance remains one of the most effective protective measures.

Shielding involves placing appropriate materials between the radiation source and the individual to reduce exposure. Its effectiveness depends on the type and energy of the radiation, as well as on the material's thickness and atomic number. Common shielding materials include lead, steel, and concrete.

In clinical environments, the exposure of healthcare personnel is mainly due to scattered radiation rather than the primary beam. Protective measures therefore include both structural shielding (e.g., walls and barriers) and personal protective equipment, such as lead aprons, thyroid shields, protective eyewear, and gloves. Additional protective devices, including ceiling-suspended screens, table-mounted curtains, and mobile shields, are commonly used, particularly in fluoroscopy and interventional procedures (IAEA, 2018; Sutton et al., 2014).

## Controlled and Supervised Areas

Workplaces where ionizing radiation is used are classified as controlled or supervised areas. This classification helps prevent unnecessary exposure and ensures that appropriate protection measures are applied.

A controlled area is a designated space where radiation exposure or the risk of contamination may be significant and where specific protective measures are required. Access is restricted and controlled, and personnel working in these areas must receive appropriate training and instructions. Warning signs, signals, and safety instructions are clearly displayed at entrances and within the area. Examples include X-ray rooms, radiotherapy treatment rooms, and nuclear medicine laboratories handling radioactive materials.

A supervised area is a location where radiation levels are lower but still require periodic monitoring and review. Special protective measures are generally not necessary; however, conditions are regularly assessed to ensure that exposure remains within acceptable limits. Examples include corridors, control rooms, and offices adjacent to controlled areas.

The clear identification of these areas helps prevent inadvertent entry and supports safe working practices (IAEA, 2014, 2018; Sutton et al., 2014).

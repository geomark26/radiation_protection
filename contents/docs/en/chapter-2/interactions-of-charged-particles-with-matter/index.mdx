---
title: "Interactions of Energetic Charged Particles with Matter"
description: "How charged particles lose energy through elastic and inelastic collisions, and the concept of particle range."
---

When an energetic charged particle travels through matter, it loses energy and slows down through multiple interactions with atoms in the medium. These interactions, governed by Coulomb forces with orbital electrons and nuclei, result in gradual energy loss and occasional changes in direction. Because each interaction transfers only a small amount of energy, the particle undergoes many interactions before coming to rest.

These interactions are referred to as collisions and are classified as either elastic or inelastic (Baeyens et al., 2023; Podgoršak, 2016).

In elastic collisions, the charged particle interacts with an atom or nucleus, primarily changing direction, with only a small transfer of energy.

In inelastic collisions, part of the particle's kinetic energy is transferred to the medium. Depending on the proximity of the interaction, these collisions are classified into three types:

1. **Hard (close) collisions:** These occur when the particle passes very close to an orbital electron, transferring a relatively large amount of energy and causing its ejection. The ejected electron may produce further ionizations and is referred to as a delta (δ) ray. Such collisions are relatively infrequent but involve significant energy transfer.

2. **Soft (distant) collisions:** These occur when the particle passes at a greater distance from the atom, transferring only a small amount of energy. They may cause excitation or ionization, typically involving outer-shell electrons. Although each interaction involves only a small energy transfer, such collisions are very numerous and account for a large fraction of the total energy loss.

3. **Radiative collisions:** These occur mainly when light-charged particles (such as electrons or positrons) interact with the Coulomb field of the nucleus and undergo deceleration. In this case, part of their energy is emitted as X-ray photons, known as bremsstrahlung (Baeyens et al., 2023; Cherry et al., 2012; Podgoršak, 2016).

Heavy charged particles, such as alpha particles and protons, lose their energy primarily through numerous inelastic collisions, transferring only a small fraction of their energy in each interaction. Because of their relatively large mass, they are only slightly deflected. As a result, they follow short, nearly straight paths and produce dense ionization along their tracks.

Light charged particles are more easily deflected due to their low mass. They may lose a large fraction of their energy in single interactions and can be scattered through large angles. Consequently, their paths are irregular (tortuous), and their range is less predictable (Cherry et al., 2012).

For charged particles, the range is defined as the average distance a particle travels in a given medium before coming to rest.

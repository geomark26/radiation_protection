title: "Interactions of High-Energy Photons with Matter"
description: "Rayleigh scattering, Compton scattering, photoelectric effect, pair production, and exponential attenuation."

When high-energy photons pass through matter, they may either pass through without interaction or interact with atoms and transfer part or all of their energy.

The interactions may result in:

- Scattering, where the photon changes direction and transfers part of its energy
- Absorption, where the photon transfers all its energy and disappears

In both cases, when energy is transferred to electrons, these electrons can produce ionizations and excitations in the material. A single energetic electron may produce hundreds or thousands of secondary ionizations (Cherry et al., 2012; Huda, 2010).

The interaction of a photon with matter is a probabilistic process, meaning that there is no certainty that an interaction will occur at a given point. Instead, only the probability of interaction can be described.

Although several types of photon-matter interactions exist, four are of particular importance in medical physics: coherent (Rayleigh) scattering, Compton scattering, the photoelectric effect, and pair production.

Each type of photon-matter interaction has a probability of occurrence that depends on the energy of the photon and the properties of the absorbing material, particularly its density and atomic number (Cherry et al., 2012; Podgoršak, 2016).

## Coherent (Rayleigh) Scattering

In coherent scattering, a low-energy photon interacts with the atom as a whole. The photon is scattered with essentially no loss of energy but changes direction by a small angle.

For the photon energies typically used in medical procedures, this type of interaction is relatively rare and contributes negligibly to radiation dose (Huda, 2010; Podgoršak, 2016).

## Compton Scattering

In Compton scattering, the incident photon interacts with a loosely bound outer-shell electron. Part of the photon's energy is transferred to the electron, which is ejected from the atom as a Compton electron, while the photon is deflected and continues with reduced energy.

The amount of energy transferred depends on the scattering angle: small angles result in low energy transfer, whereas large angles result in higher energy transfer. The ejected electron subsequently loses its energy through excitation and ionization processes in the material, while the scattered photon may undergo further interactions.

Compton scattering is a major contributor to occupational exposure due to scattered radiation (Cherry et al., 2012; Huda, 2010).

## Photoelectric Effect

In the photoelectric effect, an incident photon interacts with a tightly bound inner-shell electron and is completely absorbed, resulting in the ejection of the electron, known as a photoelectron.

For this interaction to occur, the photon energy must exceed the binding energy of the electron. The resulting vacancy in the inner shell is subsequently filled by an electron from a higher energy level, leading to the emission of characteristic X-rays or Auger electrons (Cherry et al., 2012; Huda, 2010).

## Pair Production

Pair production occurs when a high-energy photon interacts with the electric field of a nucleus. The photon is completely absorbed, and its energy is converted into an electron-positron pair.

This process can occur only if the photon energy exceeds 1.022 MeV, which corresponds to the combined rest mass energy of the electron and positron. The excess energy is shared as kinetic energy between the two particles.

After losing its kinetic energy through excitation and ionization, the positron annihilates with an electron, producing two gamma photons emitted in approximately opposite directions.

Pair production becomes important at high photon energies and is an example of mass-energy equivalence (Cherry et al., 2012; Podgoršak, 2016).

## Exponential Attenuation Law

At the macroscopic level, when a beam of photons passes through a material, its intensity decreases progressively due to interactions with the atoms of the medium. Each photon is characterized by a certain probability of interaction, and as the beam penetrates deeper into the material, the number of photons decreases.

For a narrow, monoenergetic photon beam of initial intensity I₀ transmitted through a homogeneous material, attenuation is described by the exponential attenuation law (Beer-Lambert law):

**I(x) = I₀ e<sup>−μx</sup>**

where I(x) is the beam intensity after passing through a material of thickness x.

The linear attenuation coefficient (μ) represents the probability per unit distance that a photon will undergo any type of interaction with the material. It depends on the photon energy as well as on the density and atomic number of the material.

A useful concept is the half-value layer (HVL), defined as the thickness of a material required to reduce the beam intensity to one half of its initial value. For example:

- 1 HVL → reduces intensity to 50%
- 2 HVL → reduces intensity to 25%

The HVL is widely used in radiation protection and shielding design, as it provides a simple and practical measure of how effectively a material attenuates radiation (Cherry et al., 2012; Podgoršak, 2016).

---
title: "Classification of Radiation"
description: "How radiation is classified as ionizing or non-ionizing, and directly or indirectly ionizing."
---

Depending on its ability to ionize matter, radiation is classified as either ionizing or non-ionizing.

Non-ionizing radiation has energy lower than the ionization potential of atoms or molecules and therefore cannot remove electrons from them. Examples include radio waves, microwaves, and visible light.

Ionizing radiation has sufficient energy to remove electrons from atoms or molecules, producing ionization. It is further classified as directly ionizing or indirectly ionizing, depending on how energy is transferred to matter (Podgoršak, 2016).

Directly ionizing radiation consists of charged particles, such as electrons, protons, alpha particles, and heavy ions. These particles deposit energy directly in matter through Coulomb interactions with atoms along their path.

Indirectly ionizing radiation consists of neutral particles, such as photons (X-rays and gamma rays) and neutrons. These particles do not ionize matter directly. Instead, they first transfer energy to charged particles (such as electrons or positrons), which then deposit energy in matter through direct interactions (Maqbool, 2017; Podgoršak, 2016).

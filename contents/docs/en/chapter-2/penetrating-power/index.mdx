---
title: "Penetrating Power"
description: "Comparison of the penetrating abilities of alpha particles, beta particles, and gamma rays."
---

The penetrating power of ionizing radiation refers to its ability to pass through matter without being completely absorbed. It depends on the type of radiation, as well as its energy, mass, and electric charge, and on the properties of the absorbing material, such as density and atomic number. In general, electromagnetic radiation with higher photon energy and particulate radiation with lower mass and electric charge exhibit greater penetrating power.

Alpha particles have a relatively large mass and a double positive charge, which result in strong interactions with matter and rapid energy loss. Consequently, they exhibit very low penetrating power. Their range in air is typically on the order of a few centimeters, and they can be stopped by a sheet of paper or the outer layer of human skin.

Alpha radiation can be highly hazardous if alpha-emitting substances are inhaled, ingested, or enter the body through wounds.

Beta particles (electrons and positrons) have a much lower mass and carry a single electric charge, which allows them to penetrate further than alpha particles. Their range in air may reach several meters, while in solid materials it is typically limited to a few millimeters.

Beta radiation can penetrate the skin to the germinal layer and may cause skin injury with prolonged exposure. Internal exposure to beta-emitting radionuclides can also pose significant health risks.

Gamma rays and X-rays are highly penetrating and can pass through significant thicknesses of matter. Unlike charged particles, they do not have a finite range and are attenuated exponentially as they pass through matter. They can penetrate even substantial thicknesses of materials, including the human body (Cherry et al., 2012; Podgoršak et al., 2014; United Nations Environment Programme, 2016; U.S. Department of Energy, 2012).

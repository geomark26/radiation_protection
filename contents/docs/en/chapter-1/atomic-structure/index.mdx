The Rutherford-Bohr model provides a simplified representation of atomic structure suitable for understanding radiation physics. In this model, an atom consists of a dense, positively charged nucleus surrounded by negatively charged electrons.

The nucleus is composed of protons and neutrons, while electrons occupy discrete circular orbits (shells) under the influence of electrostatic (Coulomb) forces. These shells are labeled K, L, M, and so on, starting from the innermost region.

Each shell corresponds to a specific energy level and can accommodate a limited number of electrons. The energy of electrons occupying a shell increases with increasing distance from the nucleus. Electrons in the outermost shell, known as valence electrons, are responsible for the formation of chemical bonds.

Atoms normally exist in their ground state, in which electrons occupy the lowest available energy levels.

The binding energy of an electron is the energy required to remove it from its shell to an infinite distance from the atom. Binding energy decreases with increasing distance from the nucleus and increases with the number of protons (Cherry et al., 2012; Podgoršak et al., 2014).

## Atomic Excitation and Ionization

When an atom absorbs energy, an electron may transition to a higher energy level, provided that the absorbed energy is equal to the energy difference between the two levels. The atom is then in an excited state. Because the excited state is unstable, the electron quickly returns to a lower energy level, emitting the excess energy as a photon. The energy of the emitted photon is characteristic of the element and the shells involved; therefore, the emitted radiation is referred to as characteristic radiation. When the photon energy lies within the X-ray range, it is termed characteristic X-ray emission.

If the energy imparted to the atom is sufficiently high, an electron may be completely removed from the electric field of the nucleus, leaving the atom positively charged. This process is known as ionization, and the atom is said to be ionized. The vacancy created in an inner-shell is filled by an electron from a higher energy level, leading to the emission of characteristic radiation. The minimum energy required to ionize an atom is referred to as the ionization energy or ionization potential.

As an alternative to the production of characteristic radiation, the energy released when an electron from a higher energy level fills an inner-shell vacancy may be transferred to another electron, which is subsequently ejected from the atom. The ejected electron is referred to as an Auger electron.

Ionization and excitation are the primary mechanisms through which radiation deposits energy in matter. In biological tissue, these processes initiate the sequence of events leading to cellular damage (Cember & Johnson, 2009; Cherry et al., 2012).

## Nuclear Structure

The nucleus is composed of protons and neutrons, collectively known as nucleons. Protons carry a positive charge, while neutrons are electrically neutral, resulting in a net positive nuclear charge.

The atomic number (Z) represents the number of protons in the nucleus and determines the chemical identity of the element. The mass number (A) is the total number of nucleons. In a neutral atom, the number of electrons equals the number of protons.

Nucleons are held together by the strong nuclear force, which overcomes the electrostatic repulsion between positively charged protons.

The shell model of the nucleus proposes that protons and neutrons move in complex ways under nuclear forces. According to this model, nucleons occupy specific allowed "orbits," similar to how electrons occupy allowed orbits around the nucleus. The most stable configuration is the ground state, while higher-energy configurations correspond to excited or metastable states. Excited states exist for extremely short times, whereas metastable states may persist significantly longer by nuclear standards, often up to hours (Cherry et al., 2012; Podgoršak, 2016).

Nuclei that have the same number of protons but differ in the number of neutrons are classified as isotopes. A nuclide is a nuclear species characterized by its specific nuclear composition (A and Z) and the arrangement of nucleons within the nucleus. Metastable forms are treated as distinct nuclides and are referred to as nuclear isomers (Cember & Johnson, 2009; Cherry et al., 2012).

## Nuclear Stability

Not all arrangements of protons and neutrons result in stable nuclei. Observations of naturally occurring elements show that stable nuclei cluster around a so-called "line of stability," which reflects the most favorable neutron-to-proton ratios. For lighter elements, stability occurs when the number of neutrons is approximately equal to the number of protons. Heavier nuclei require a higher proportion of neutrons (approximately 50% more) to remain stable.

Another important indicator of stability is the binding energy per nucleon. Higher values indicate stronger nuclear cohesion and greater stability. This value reaches a maximum near mass number 60 and decreases for heavier nuclei, reflecting the increasing instability associated with large atomic masses (Cherry et al., 2012).

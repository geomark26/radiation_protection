---
title: "Quantities and Units"
description: "Absorbed dose, dose rate, and linear energy transfer (LET) as fundamental radiation measurement quantities."
---

To quantify the energy deposited by ionizing radiation in a material, such as biological tissue, the quantity absorbed dose (or simply dose) is used. It is defined as the ratio of the mean energy imparted to a material to its mass. The unit of absorbed dose is the gray (Gy).

The biological effects of radiation depend not only on the absorbed dose but also on the rate at which energy is deposited. Dose rate is a related quantity, defined as the absorbed dose delivered per unit time. It is commonly expressed in units of gray per hour (Gy·h⁻¹).

Another important quantity is linear energy transfer (LET), which describes how densely energy is deposited along the path of radiation. LET is defined as the average energy transferred per unit length of the radiation track.

Radiation can be broadly classified based on LET:

- Low-LET radiation, such as X-rays and gamma rays, produces sparsely distributed ionizations along its path
- High-LET radiation, such as protons and heavy ions, produces dense ionization along short tracks

High-LET radiation generally produces more complex and less repairable biological damage compared with low-LET radiation (Baeyens et al., 2023).

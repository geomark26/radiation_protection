---
title: The System of Radiation Protection
description: Framework of principles, regulations, and practical measures for radiation protection.
---

The system of radiation protection is a structured framework of principles, regulations, and practical measures designed to protect individuals, society, and the environment from the harmful effects of ionizing radiation. By balancing potential risks against anticipated benefits, the system ensures the justified use of ionizing radiation in medicine, industry, research, and other applications. Its main goal is to prevent deterministic effects and to reduce the probability of stochastic effects to acceptable levels.

The scientific basis of radiation protection is primarily provided by the United Nations Scientific Committee on the Effects of Atomic Radiation (UNSCEAR), which evaluates data from epidemiological studies, experimental research, and environmental measurements to assess radiation risks.

Based on scientific evidence, the International Commission on Radiological Protection (ICRP) develops recommendations that define the fundamental principles of radiation protection (justification, optimization, and dose limitation) as well as key protection quantities and dose limits.

The International Atomic Energy Agency (IAEA), drawing upon the scientific findings of UNSCEAR and the recommendations of international expert bodies, particularly the ICRP, publishes the International Safety Standards. These standards support countries in establishing and maintaining effective regulatory systems.

Within the European Union, Council Directive 2013/59/Euratom establishes legally binding requirements for radiation protection.

In Greece, these requirements are incorporated into national legislation, and the Greek Atomic Energy Commission is the competent authority responsible for the regulation and supervision of radiological and nuclear safety, as well as radiation protection.

The system of radiation protection applies to all exposure situations (planned, emergency, and existing) and to all categories of exposure (occupational, medical, and public). Its effective implementation depends on the cooperation of regulatory authorities, employers, radiation protection experts, and workers (ICRP, 2007; IAEA, 2014; Council of the European Union, 2013; EEAE, n.d.).

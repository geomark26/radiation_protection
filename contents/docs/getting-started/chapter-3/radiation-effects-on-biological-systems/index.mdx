Ionizing radiation interacts with biological tissue primarily by transferring energy to atoms and molecules, causing ionization and excitation. These initial physical events occur within extremely short time scales and initiate a sequence of chemical and biological processes that may ultimately result in cellular damage.

## Direct and Indirect Action

Ionizing radiation can damage critical biomolecules, particularly DNA, through two main mechanisms: direct action and indirect action.

In direct action, radiation deposits energy directly in a critical biological molecule, such as DNA, causing ionization or excitation of its atoms. This may result in the breaking of chemical bonds and the formation of free radicals within the molecule itself.

Free radicals are ions, atoms or molecules with one or more unpaired electrons, making them highly reactive and chemically unstable. When formed within DNA, they can alter its molecular structure and impair its function. Direct action is the predominant mechanism for high-LET radiation, which deposits energy densely along its track (Baeyens et al., 2023; IAEA, 2010).

In indirect action, radiation interacts with molecules other than critical targets in the cell, most importantly water, which constitutes the majority of cellular content. This interaction leads to the formation of free radicals that can diffuse over short distances and react with critical biomolecules, including DNA, proteins, and lipids, causing damage. Indirect action is the predominant mechanism for low-LET radiation.

The process of indirect action in water, known as water radiolysis, can be described in four phases:

- Physical phase: Energy is deposited in water molecules, causing ionization and excitation
- Physicochemical phase: Formation of free radicals
- Chemical phase: Free radicals diffuse and interact with nearby biomolecules, inducing structural damage
- Biological phase: The chemical damage is expressed as biological effects such as DNA damage, cellular dysfunction, or cell death

The biological phase may extend from minutes to years, depending on cellular conditions and repair processes (Baeyens et al., 2023; IAEA, 2010).

## DNA Damage

Although ionizing radiation can damage all cellular components, DNA is considered the most critical target because it carries the genetic information required for cell survival and replication.

DNA lesions include base alterations, single- and double-strand breaks, DNA cross-links, and complex DNA damage:

- **Base lesions:** Modification or loss of DNA bases, which may alter the genetic code; in some cases, a base may be completely removed, forming an abasic site
- **Single-strand breaks (SSBs):** Breaks affecting one DNA strand; these are relatively common and usually repaired efficiently
- **Double-strand breaks (DSBs):** Breaks affecting both DNA strands in close proximity; these are the most severe lesions and are strongly associated with cell death, mutations, and carcinogenesis
- **DNA cross-links:** Bonds formed within the same strand (intrastrand), between strands (interstrand), or between DNA and proteins, interfering with replication and transcription
- **Clustered DNA damage:** Multiple closely spaced lesions within a short DNA segment, which are particularly difficult to repair (Reindl et al., 2023; Wondergem, 2014)

## DNA Repair and Cellular Response to Ionizing Radiation

Exposure to ionizing radiation can induce a large number of DNA lesions. However, cells possess efficient repair mechanisms, involving specialized enzymatic pathways, that detect and repair most of this damage. Only a small fraction of radiation-induced DNA damage remains unrepaired or is repaired incorrectly, leading to biological consequences (Dale & Wondergem, 2014; Wondergem, 2014).

Three main outcomes of DNA damage can be distinguished: accurate repair, cell death, and misrepair.

When DNA damage is repaired accurately, the original genetic information is restored, and the cell continues to function normally. Such cells retain their ability to divide and are referred to as clonogenic cells (Suntharalingam et al., 2005).

Conversely, when the damage is extensive or repair mechanisms fail, the cell loses its functionality and/or its ability to divide, resulting in cell death. In non-proliferating cells, cell death is associated with the loss of metabolic activity, whereas in proliferating cells it is defined as the loss of reproductive capacity.

Pathways of cell death include:

- **Apoptosis:** A regulated, programmed process in which the cell eliminates itself in response to severe damage; common in radiosensitive tissues
- **Necrosis:** An unregulated form of cell death resulting from severe damage to cellular structures, often associated with inflammation
- **Mitotic catastrophe:** Cell death occurring after defective mitosis due to unrepaired DNA damage

Cellular senescence represents a non-lethal outcome characterized by permanent growth arrest, in which cells remain metabolically active but are no longer capable of proliferation (Joiner, 2009; Reindl et al., 2023; Wondergem, 2014).

When DNA damage is repaired incorrectly (misrepair), the cell survives but carries permanent genetic alterations, such as mutations or chromosomal aberrations. These changes may be transmitted to daughter cells, leading to genomic instability and increasing the risk of cancer development. This outcome is particularly important in proliferating cells, such as stem cells or germ cells (Suntharalingam et al., 2005).

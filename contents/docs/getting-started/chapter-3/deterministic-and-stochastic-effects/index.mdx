---
title: "Deterministic and Stochastic Effects"
description: "Threshold-based deterministic effects and probabilistic stochastic effects of ionizing radiation exposure."
---

Exposure to ionizing radiation can result in a range of biological effects. These effects are classified as somatic when they occur in the exposed individual, or hereditary when they affect future generations through damage to germ cells. Somatic effects are further divided into deterministic and stochastic effects, while hereditary effects are considered stochastic in nature (Wondergem, 2014).

## Deterministic Effects

Deterministic effects, or tissue reactions, occur only when the absorbed dose exceeds a defined threshold, below which no clinically detectable effects are observed. Above this threshold, the severity of the effects increases with increasing dose. These effects are associated with the loss of a large number of cells, particularly stem cells, resulting in impaired tissue function when the tissue's repair capacity is exceeded. Their manifestation may occur over a time span ranging from hours to years following exposure (Baeyens et al., 2023; Wondergem, 2014).

A characteristic example is the acute radiation syndrome (ARS), which occurs following high-dose, whole-body or near whole-body exposure, typically above ~1 Gy. ARS develops in stages:

- Prodromal phase: Early symptoms such as nausea, vomiting, and fatigue
- Latent phase: Temporary improvement of symptoms despite ongoing damage
- Manifest illness phase: Clinical effects depending on dose, including:
  - Hematopoietic syndrome: bone marrow suppression, infections, bleeding
  - Gastrointestinal syndrome: severe diarrhea, dehydration, sepsis
  - Central nervous system syndrome: at very high doses, leading to rapid deterioration and death

Recovery depends on the survival of stem cells and the effectiveness of medical care.

Deterministic effects may also result from localized exposure. Examples include skin reactions (erythema, epilation, necrosis), cataract formation, temporary or permanent sterility and late tissue effects such as fibrosis or organ dysfunction (Baeyens et al., 2023; Saha, 2019).

## Stochastic Effects

Stochastic effects do not have a threshold, and their occurrence is probabilistic. The probability of occurrence increases with dose, but the severity of the effect is independent of the dose. These effects typically appear long after exposure (Wondergem, 2014).

The primary stochastic effect is cancer. Radiation-induced cancer arises from genetic alterations that lead to uncontrolled cellular proliferation. The risk of its occurrence increases with dose and is influenced by various factors, such as individual susceptibility and environmental conditions.

Because cancer has many possible causes, radiation exposure cannot usually be identified as the direct cause in an individual case. Instead, radiation effects are evaluated by observing increased cancer incidence in exposed populations compared with baseline levels. Current epidemiological evidence suggests that an increased cancer risk can be reliably detected at doses of approximately 0.1 Gy or higher (Baeyens et al., 2023; IAEA, 2010).

Hereditary effects arise from radiation-induced damage to the DNA of germ cells (sperm or ova), which may be transmitted to offspring. Although such effects have been demonstrated in experimental animals, there is currently no conclusive evidence of increased hereditary effects in human populations exposed to ionizing radiation (Baeyens et al., 2023; UNSCEAR, 2008).

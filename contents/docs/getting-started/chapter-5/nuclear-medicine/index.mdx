Nuclear medicine is a medical specialty that uses unsealed radioactive materials, known as radiopharmaceuticals, for diagnostic and therapeutic purposes. These substances consist of radionuclides bound to pharmaceutical compounds that allow them to localize in specific organs or tissues.

Radiopharmaceuticals are typically administered via oral, intravenous, or intracavitary routes. Following administration, the patient becomes a source of radiation. These substances are excreted primarily through urine, feces, sweat, and other bodily fluids, which may become sources of contamination and radiation exposure.

## Diagnostic Nuclear Medicine Procedures

Diagnostic procedures involve the administration of small amounts of short-lived radiopharmaceuticals, typically emitting gamma rays or positrons. The radiopharmaceutical is distributed within the patient's body and emits radiation, which is detected to provide functional and metabolic information about organs, tissues, and physiological processes.

Radiation exposure to personnel and the public from diagnostic nuclear medicine procedures is generally low. Patients are typically discharged shortly after radiopharmaceutical administration, and only limited radiation protection instructions are required, as the administered activities result in relatively low radiation levels.

The main imaging systems used in nuclear medicine are gamma camera systems (including single-photon emission computed tomography, SPECT) and positron emission tomography (PET) (Flux & Yong, 2014; Lodge & Frey, 2014; Saha, 2019).

## Therapeutic Nuclear Medicine Procedures

Therapeutic procedures involve the administration of higher-activity radiopharmaceuticals, often emitting beta particles that deliver radiation directly to target tissues. Beta decay may also be accompanied by the emission of gamma rays and/or characteristic X-rays, which can penetrate the body and contribute to external exposure.

These procedures are generally performed in hospital settings. Depending on the radionuclide and the administered activity, patients may remain radioactive for extended periods and may require hospitalization in dedicated isolation rooms designated as controlled areas with restricted access.

Hospitalization ensures that family members, caregivers, and the public are protected from unnecessary exposure. Once the residual activity falls below established release criteria, patients may be discharged with specific radiation protection instructions (IAEA, 2009; Saha, 2019).

## Radiation Protection Measures in Nuclear Medicine

In nuclear medicine, exposure may occur through two main pathways:

- External exposure, originating from radioactive sources, including patients who have been administered radiopharmaceuticals
- Internal exposure (contamination), resulting from the intake of radioactive material through inhalation, ingestion, skin contamination, or absorption through wounds

When appropriate radiation protection measures are implemented, exposure to non-radiation healthcare workers is generally minimal. However, exposure may become significant under certain circumstances, such as accidental contamination events, emergency medical care, urgent surgery involving patients who have recently received therapeutic doses of radiopharmaceuticals, or patient transport (Flux & Yong, 2014; Lodge & Frey, 2014).

### General Protection Principles and Safety Precautions

The principles of time, distance, and shielding apply:

- Minimize time spent near radioactive sources or patients
- Maximize distance whenever possible
- Use shielding where available

Structural shielding is effective; however, lead aprons provide limited protection against high-energy gamma emitters (e.g., iodine-131), although portable shielding may be useful. Personal protective equipment, such as gloves, gowns, eye protection, masks, and shoe covers, is important for preventing contamination.

Personnel who are or may be pregnant should avoid close contact with patients who have recently received therapeutic levels of radiopharmaceuticals.

Radiation warning signs are present in areas where radioactive materials are used, stored, or disposed of. Examples include nuclear medicine facilities, radioactive waste storage areas, and containers holding contaminated materials (Carlsson & Le Heron, 2014; Saha, 2019). The following precautions should be observed:

- Enter controlled areas only with authorization and appropriate instructions
- Follow all posted safety instructions
- Do not handle unknown or suspicious materials

### Contamination

Contamination is the presence of radioactive material on surfaces, clothing, skin, or within the body. It may occur due to the spillage of radiopharmaceuticals or biological fluids from patients (e.g., urine, vomit, blood).

Contamination may be classified as external, when radioactive material is present on the skin, clothing, or surfaces, or internal, when radioactive material enters the body.

The severity of contamination depends on several factors, including the radionuclide type and activity, the extent of spread, the number of individuals involved, and the radiotoxicity of the material (U.S. Department of Energy, 2012; Saha, 2019).

In the event of contamination (e.g., accidental release of radioactive material, such as radiopharmaceuticals or patient bodily fluids), the following actions should be taken:

- Avoid direct contact with the radioactive material
- Warn others and restrict access to the area
- Wear protective clothing and disposable gloves, if available
- Cover the spill with absorbent materials to prevent the spread of contamination
- Notify the Radiation Protection Officer (RPO) or nuclear medicine staff immediately, and follow their instructions
- In cases of severe contamination, ensure that decontamination is carried out under the supervision of the RPO

In case of personal contamination:

- If skin contamination occurs, the affected area should be washed immediately with non-abrasive soap and cold water
- If eye contamination occurs, the eye should be flushed immediately with large quantities of water
- Inform radiation protection personnel after initial decontamination (Saha, 2019; IAEA, 2018; IAEA, 2005)

### Medical Emergencies

Life-threatening medical emergencies (e.g., cardiac arrest, stroke) involving radioactive patients must never be delayed due to radiation concerns. Emergency personnel should be appropriately trained in the management of radioactive patients. In such situations, the following precautions should be observed:

- Inform healthcare staff when a radioactive patient is admitted
- Wear impermeable protective gloves
- Avoid direct contact with bodily fluids
- Take precautions to prevent contamination

If the medical condition of a radioactive patient deteriorates and transfer to an intensive care unit or cardiac care unit is required, the RPO should be informed immediately to provide specific radiation protection instructions.

If urgent surgery is required, the RPO should also be informed immediately. Operating room personnel should:

- Be informed of the patient's radioactive status
- Follow the instructions of the RPO, which may modify operating procedures to minimize exposure and the spread of contamination
- Wear appropriate protective equipment, provided that it does not compromise surgical performance
- Remain in the operating room only for the time necessary to perform their duties
- Be rotated if the surgical procedure is prolonged (IAEA, 2005; Dauer, 2014)

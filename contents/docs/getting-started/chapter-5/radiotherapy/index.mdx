Radiotherapy is a medical specialty that uses high doses of ionizing radiation to treat cancer and, in some cases, non-malignant diseases. The objective is to deliver a therapeutic dose to the target tissue while minimizing exposure to surrounding healthy tissues. Two main categories are used: external beam radiotherapy and brachytherapy.

In external beam radiotherapy, radiation is delivered from outside the body using high-energy X-rays, gamma rays, electrons, or heavy charged particles. These beams are produced by specialized equipment such as linear accelerators or radioactive sources (e.g., cobalt-60).

In brachytherapy, small sealed radioactive sources are placed directly inside or near the tumor, allowing the delivery of high doses locally while reducing exposure to surrounding tissues (IAEA, 2018; Suntharalingam, 2005; ICRP, 2021).

## Radiation Protection Measures in External Beam Therapy

Radiotherapy treatment rooms are controlled areas with restricted access, warning signs, and safety systems. Due to the high-energy beams used, no individual should be present in the treatment room during radiation delivery, except for the patient.

Safety systems such as interlocks are installed to immediately interrupt radiation delivery if necessary. Interlocks, located within the treatment room, allow manual interruption of the treatment machine in cases where someone has accidentally remained in the room during treatment delivery (IAEA, 2018).

Safety precautions:

- Follow instructions from radiotherapy staff
- Follow all warning signs and light signals
- Enter the treatment room only when authorized and instructed by radiotherapy staff

## Radiation Protection Measures in Brachytherapy

Brachytherapy treatments are classified as permanent or temporary implantation techniques.

In permanent implantation, small radioactive sources remain in the target tissue and gradually decay over time.

In temporary implantation, radioactive sources are inserted for a defined period and subsequently removed. Temporary techniques include high-dose-rate (HDR) brachytherapy, in which the dose is delivered at a high rate over a short period (minutes); low-dose-rate (LDR) brachytherapy, in which the dose is delivered continuously at a low rate over a longer period (hours to days); and pulse-dose-rate (PDR) brachytherapy, in which the dose is delivered in periodic pulses to simulate continuous LDR treatment.

In HDR and PDR brachytherapy, patients are radioactive only during the treatment. In LDR brachytherapy, patients remain radioactive for the duration required for treatment (hours to days) and may be hospitalized in dedicated controlled areas.

Brachytherapy sources are typically very small and may be difficult to distinguish from common medical materials. Although rare, incidents such as displacement or loss of a radioactive source may occur, particularly in LDR treatments, and may lead to significant radiation exposure (IAEA, 2018; ICRP, 2021; Suntharalingam, 2005).

Any unfamiliar object found near a brachytherapy patient (e.g., in bedding, clothing, or waste) should be treated as potentially radioactive. Contact should be avoided, and designated radiation protection personnel should be notified immediately.

Safety precautions:

- Follow instructions from radiotherapy staff
- Limit time spent near a radioactive patient
- Maintain distance whenever possible
- Do not touch or handle any unfamiliar objects near the patient
- Report any suspected radioactive object immediately
